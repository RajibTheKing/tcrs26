\section{Experiment and Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}
    The train client based on Lingua Franca was deployed on a Raspberry Pi 5, which coordinates the test vehicle equipped with a camera and a motor controller. The central server was deployed on a cloud instance hosted in Frankfurt, Germany. During the experiments, the test vehicle was located in Lütjenburg, Germany, approximately 483 km from Frankfurt, and was controlled remotely from the laboratory at Kiel University, approximately 480 km from Frankfurt, using the remote control interface. The test vehicle was approximately 31 km away from the remote control setup. In these experiments, the train client was connected to the central server over QUIC protocol via 5G network using a Chateau 5G R17 AX router, while the remote control client was connected to the central server over WebTransport protocol using wired Ethernet cable.     German regulations require glass-to-glass latency for control commands and video transmission in remotely operated vehicles to remain below 200 ms \cite{StVFernLV2025}. For each video frame, the total delay, including image capture, encoding, network transmission, decoding, and rendering, must therefore not exceed 200 ms. No specific German regulation exists for remotely controlled trains so far, but we assume that our system should meet similar requirement. Modern GPUs can encode single 4K video frame in approximately 15.8ms \cite{ArunruangsirilertK24}. In our experiments, we used 1280×720 video stream at 30 FPS with a 3 Mbps bitrate. Based on the performance of modern hardware, we assume that all computationally intensive processing stages can be completed within approximately 50 ms per frame. Our evaluation focuses primarily on latency introduced by realistic network conditions. We are presenting the results of one trip session starting from Friederikenthal to Lütjenburg, traveling a distance of 1781 meters with a duration of 580 seconds.

    The left panel of Figure \ref{fig:latency_measurements} presents the end-to-end latency measurements for control commands, while the right panel shows the rolling average latency for video frames, calculated with a window size of 30 frames. The results indicate that all control commands were received within acceptable limits. Additionally, no control commands were missed during the trip sessions. Consequently, the reliable channel enabled by the QUIC-based WebTransport protocol is suitable for transmitting control commands in remote train control systems.

    In the results, we observed that 17,387 video frames were received and 44 video frames were missed during the trip session, which is expected under real-world network conditions. The rolling average latency of the video stream indicates that frames were mostly received within acceptable limits. However, there were a few instances where latency exceeded the threshold. In such cases, the remote control interface can be designed to provide immediate alerts to the operator, indicating that the video stream is experiencing delays and enabling appropriate corrective action. Overall, the results demonstrate that the system is capable of providing low-latency remote control of trains over WebTransport.
