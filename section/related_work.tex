\subsection{Remote Control Systems for Automated Railways}

As railway systems advance toward higher levels of automation (GoA 3 and 4), the role of remote control as a safety-critical fallback mechanism has gained significant attention. Cogan-Milius et al. \cite{CoganMilius2023} conducted a comprehensive study investigating the needs and preferences of future operators for remote control systems in automated trains. Their research highlights that while full automation aims to reduce human involvement, remote control capabilities remain essential for handling edge cases, system degradation, and emergency situations where human judgment and intervention are necessary. The study emphasizes that operators require intuitive interfaces, reliable communication channels, and situational awareness tools to effectively monitor and control trains remotely when automation systems fail or encounter unprecedented scenarios. These findings underscore the importance of designing remote control systems that not only provide technical capabilities but also align with human factors and operational workflows—a consideration that directly influences the architectural and interface design decisions in modern remote control platforms.

\subsection{QUIC Protocol Performance}

The QUIC protocol, standardized as the foundation for HTTP/3, has emerged as a promising alternative to traditional TCP-based and UDP-based communication for real-time applications. A comprehensive performance evaluation conducted in 2024 \cite{10.1145/3696355.3699698} analyzed QUIC's behavior in real-time network environments, examining key metrics such as latency, throughput, connection establishment times, and resilience to packet loss. The study demonstrated that QUIC's integration of transport and cryptographic handshakes significantly reduces connection setup time compared to TCP+TLS, achieving near-zero round-trip time (0-RTT) connection resumption in many scenarios. Furthermore, the evaluation revealed that QUIC's built-in congestion control mechanisms and multiplexed stream architecture effectively mitigate head-of-line blocking issues that plague TCP-based protocols, particularly in lossy network conditions. These characteristics are particularly relevant for railway remote control applications where rapid connection establishment, low latency, and resilience to intermittent connectivity are critical requirements. The findings provide empirical evidence supporting QUIC as a suitable transport layer for safety-critical real-time control systems, validating its adoption in next-generation railway communication architectures.
